# Assignment Rubric

Use this reference when evaluating a candidate's written assignment, homework task, case task, research note, company research document, sourcing exercise, or other screening exercise.

## Evaluation Dimensions

## Current TTC OpenAI Research Assignment Weighting

For the current "OpenAI company research from a headhunter perspective" assignment, judge the submitted document mainly by effort, completeness, information reliability, structure, and the candidate's own judgment. AI use quality is a supporting signal, not the main driver. Do not use this assignment rubric to re-score phone-screen performance.

Recommended rough weighting for this assignment:

- Seriousness and effort: 25%
- Coverage and completeness: 25%
- Information truthfulness and reliability: 25%
- Structure, readability, and consultant-style communication: 15%
- AI tool use quality: 10%

### Seriousness And Effort

Strong signals:

- The document looks intentionally prepared rather than quickly generated.
- Includes candidate's own synthesis, judgment, or notes rather than generic AI prose.
- Shows care in formatting, source handling, examples, and final review.

Risks:

- Looks pasted from AI with little editing.
- Very short, generic, or obviously rushed.
- Contains careless formatting, repeated content, or unresolved placeholders.

### Coverage And Completeness

Strong signals:

- Covers company overview, products/business, industry/competition, talent needs, target candidate profiles, sourcing directions, pitch, risks, and conclusion when relevant.
- Addresses the headhunter perspective rather than only writing a company introduction.
- Includes enough concrete evidence for a leader to understand the candidate's research process.

Risks:

- Misses major required sections.
- Only summarizes OpenAI at a high level.
- Does not connect company research to hiring, talent, or sourcing.

### AI Tool Use Quality

Strong signals:

- Explains which AI tools were used and how they helped.
- Uses AI for research organization, comparison, outline, synthesis, or polishing while still showing human judgment.
- Checks facts, avoids hallucinations, and adapts AI output to the headhunter task.
- Treats AI as an assistant, not a replacement for judgment, source checking, and final ownership.

Risks:

- Only says "used AI" without process detail.
- Output appears fully AI-generated and generic.
- Contains unsupported claims, hallucinations, or inconsistent facts.
- Uses many AI-related terms but cannot show what was personally checked, selected, or improved.

### Information Truthfulness And Reliability

Strong signals:

- Uses current, specific, and verifiable information.
- Cites or names credible sources such as OpenAI official pages, reputable news, company/product pages, job postings, research papers, or industry reports.
- Distinguishes confirmed facts from inference or personal judgment.
- Avoids outdated claims, fake metrics, fabricated rankings, or unsupported conclusions.

Risks:

- Includes factual errors, hallucinated numbers, fake citations, or unverifiable claims.
- Treats AI-generated statements as facts without checking.
- Uses vague phrases such as "industry-leading" or "rapidly growing" without evidence.
- Fails to separate public facts from the candidate's own assumptions.

### Completion And Timeliness

Strong signals:

- Submitted on time or proactively communicated delays.
- Completed all required parts.
- Followed format and delivery instructions.

Risks:

- Late without explanation.
- Missing required sections.
- Did not follow basic instructions.

### Understanding Of The Task

Strong signals:

- Correctly identifies the goal of the task.
- Addresses the real business or role problem rather than giving generic content.
- Makes reasonable assumptions explicit.

Risks:

- Misunderstands the task.
- Gives broad, empty, or copied content.
- Avoids the hard part of the assignment.

### Research And Information Quality

Strong signals:

- Uses relevant, specific, and verifiable information.
- Shows company, industry, market, user, candidate, or role understanding when relevant.
- Distinguishes facts from assumptions.

Risks:

- Uses unverifiable claims.
- Has obvious factual mistakes.
- Relies on shallow search results without synthesis.

### Industry Insight And Judgment

Strong signals:

- Goes beyond summarization and gives a point of view.
- Identifies important industry dynamics, risks, opportunities, or talent implications.
- Connects research findings to the role or recruiting context.

Risks:

- Only lists facts without interpretation.
- Uses generic commentary that could apply to any company or industry.
- Misses obvious strategic or talent-market implications.

### Structured Thinking

Strong signals:

- Clear logic and readable structure.
- Prioritizes key findings or actions.
- Explains why the recommendation follows from the evidence.

Risks:

- Disorganized output.
- No prioritization.
- Conclusions do not follow from evidence.

### Execution Detail

Strong signals:

- Shows attention to detail.
- Uses concrete examples, criteria, or next steps.
- Output could be used or iterated by the team.

Risks:

- Many careless mistakes.
- Too vague to use.
- Missing practical next steps.

### Communication Quality

Strong signals:

- Concise, professional writing.
- Easy for a leader or teammate to scan.
- Tone fits the role and audience.
- Shows consultant-style communication: prioritizes useful conclusions, explains implications, and avoids empty jargon.

Risks:

- Overly casual, inflated, or hard to read.
- Long without substance.
- Unclear ownership of the answer.

### AI Tool Use

Strong signals:

- Uses AI tools transparently and improves the output with human judgment.
- Can explain how the answer was produced.
- Checks facts and adapts generated content.

Risks:

- Looks fully AI-generated with little personal judgment.
- Contains hallucinations or inconsistent claims.
- Cannot explain the output if asked.

## Recommendation Levels

- `strong pass`: Assignment clearly strengthens the candidate's case.
- `pass`: Meets expectations; move forward if phone screen and availability are also acceptable.
- `borderline`: Some useful signals, but needs leader review or follow-up questions.
- `fail`: Does not meet the assignment bar or reveals major mismatch.
- `needs more info`: Evidence is incomplete; ask for clarification before deciding.

## Output Rule

When evaluating an assignment, include:

- Assignment level.
- 2-4 recommendation points with evidence.
- 1-3 risk points with evidence.
- Follow-up questions for interview or leader check.
- Suggested tracker value for `Assignment status`.
